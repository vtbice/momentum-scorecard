The City of the Future

Micro Cap is where the venture-style bets live. Around 80 names that are still small enough to be unknown — many under $2 billion in market cap — and many of them in the early innings of their stories. The risk is real and the volatility is real. The upside, when a thesis works, is also real.

Several themes run through the portfolio. The Clinic is unusually deep here, with companies redefining how disease is detected: GeneDx in pediatric whole-genome sequencing, BillionToOne in molecular prenatal and oncology testing, CeriBell in point-of-care EEG, HeartFlow in non-invasive cardiac imaging, Twist Bioscience in synthetic DNA, 10x Genomics in single-cell sequencing. The Research Labs holds early-stage biotechs that could be the next Madrigal or Revolution Medicines if the science works out — Mineralys, NewAmsterdam, Praxis, Forte. The Arsenal owns the small defense and space names with outsized growth (Ondas, BlackSky, Karman, Rocket Lab) and the Power Plant has the hardest-to-replicate clean energy and nuclear plays (Bloom, Centrus, Cipher, TeraWulf, Hut 8).

Many micro caps in this fund will not become mid caps. That is the nature of the asset class. The ones that do tend to compound at rates you cannot find anywhere else, and the guide exists to help remember why each one is in the portfolio in the first place.

The Chip Works 2

Where silicon begins — and where rare earths come from

Every city starts with a foundry. In this one, the foundry makes silicon. Modern chips have features measured in billionths of a meter — structures so small that a single virus would tower over them like a skyscraper. Building something at that scale requires extraordinary precision at every step, and the Chip Works is the district where it happens.

It begins in the ground. MP Materials (MP) mines and processes rare-earth elements — the minerals that create the powerful permanent magnets inside electric car motors, wind turbines, fighter jets, and chip-making equipment itself. MP is the only large-scale rare-earth producer in the Western Hemisphere, which makes it a strategic asset as well as a commercial one.

Inside the fab, Nova (NVMI) builds the inspection tools that photograph microscopic chip layers with beams of light and electrons, catching defects invisible to any human eye. MKS Instruments (MKSI) controls the gases, temperatures, and energy inside the fabrication chambers — imagine maintaining perfect weather inside a sealed room where a single stray particle could ruin everything. Amkor (AMKR) handles the final step, taking a finished chip thinner than a fingernail and encasing it in a protective housing with hundreds of tiny electrical connections so it can survive the heat and vibration of a working computer. Lattice Semiconductor (LSCC) makes programmable chips that can be reconfigured after installation — a toolbox that transforms into whatever tool you need at the moment. And Rambus (RMBS) designs the rules that govern how processors and memory chips talk to each other, which is another way of saying Rambus decides how fast the entire computer can think. Without these six companies, the AI revolution would be a slideshow.

AEHR Aehr Test Systems

Burn-in test systems for power semiconductors — critical for silicon carbide chips in EVs.

Aehr Test Systems builds the wafer-level burn-in systems that semiconductor companies use to stress-test chips before shipping them. Their FOX systems became essential as silicon carbide (SiC) power chips for electric vehicles took off — SiC chips are expensive and field failures are unacceptable, so manufacturers test every wafer. Aehr is small but dominant in its niche.

“Every silicon carbide chip in an EV gets stress-tested on an Aehr machine.”

The Wires 3

The interconnect that lets the city talk to itself

A chip by itself is useless. Chips only matter when they can talk to each other — and in the era of AI, talking means moving vast amounts of data at extraordinary speeds between racks, between buildings, between cities, and across oceans. The Wires is the district where signals travel.

Credo Technology (CRDO) builds the high-speed cables and connection chips that let thousands of AI processors pass notes at billions of cycles per second. Picture a room full of brilliant people trying to solve a problem together, but they can only communicate by passing notes — the speed of those notes determines how fast the group can think. Credo makes the notes move at light speed. Astera Labs (ALAB) controls the data flows inside a single rack the way traffic cops manage a highway interchange, preventing collisions and making sure nothing gets stuck. MACOM (MTSI) turns electrical signals into laser light and back again so data can ride glass fibers across continents, billions of times per second.

SiTime (SITM) makes the precision silicon clocks that keep every signal perfectly synchronized — the conductor's baton inside every electronic orchestra. Semtech (SMTC) cleans up degraded signals so data arrives crisp instead of fuzzy, like preventing a photocopy-of-a-photocopy from losing quality with every pass. And Allegro MicroSystems (ALGM) builds the sensors that measure exactly how fast things are moving inside EVs, factory robots, and data-center cooling fans so control systems can respond in milliseconds. None of these companies make headlines. All of them are essential.

AAOI Applied Optoelectronics

Fiber-optic transceivers and components for data centers and broadband networks.

Applied Optoelectronics designs and manufactures the fiber-optic modules that data centers use to connect servers and switches at very high speeds. The company has been winning new design wins for next-generation 800G transceivers as hyperscalers upgrade their networks to handle AI traffic.

“When data needs to ride a beam of light across a data center, AAOI makes the converter.”

The Machine Shop 4

Precision parts, specialty metals, and the hidden hands that assemble the city

Chips and wires are useful only if someone can turn them into physical things — airplanes, satellites, medical devices, data-center cooling loops, circuit boards, power supplies. The Machine Shop is the district where raw materials and components become real machines. These are the hidden hands that turn brilliant designs into actual products.

Carpenter Technology (CRS) forges specialty metals and advanced alloys engineered at the atomic level to withstand 2,000-degree temperatures, corrosive chemicals, and thousands of pounds of pressure. These go into jet-engine turbine blades, surgical implants, and nuclear reactor components — applications where a material failure isn't an inconvenience but a catastrophe. RBC Bearings (RBC) makes the precision bearings that enable smooth rotation inside helicopter rotors, submarine propulsion systems, and industrial machinery — a deceptively simple concept, engineered to survive where nothing else can.

Sanmina (SANM) handles complex electronic contract manufacturing: the densely packed circuit boards inside medical imaging machines, the communication systems inside military vehicles, the server boards that run cloud computing. Fabrinet (FN) specializes in optical components aligned to tolerances thinner than a wavelength of light. TTM Technologies (TTMI) is one of the top five PCB manufacturers in the world and the largest supplier of printed circuit boards to the U.S. military, and the fastest-growing segment of its business is now data center computing and AI accelerators. Modine Manufacturing (MOD) designs the liquid cooling systems that absorb heat from AI racks the way a car radiator cools an engine — transforming a legacy auto-parts company into an AI infrastructure play. And Bel Fuse (BELFB) makes the power supplies and connectivity components that quietly sit inside almost every piece of essential equipment.

The last group is about control. Woodward (WWD) designs the fuel-metering control systems inside aircraft engines and industrial turbines — what happens when a pilot pushes the throttle forward is Woodward's responsibility. Crane Company (CR) supplies the engineered valves, sensors, and fluid-handling systems used in aircraft, naval vessels, and factories. And Littelfuse (LFUS) makes circuit-protection components that sacrifice themselves during power surges, absorbing excess energy before expensive equipment is damaged. Every physical machine in the city passes through a shop like one of these.

The Construction Yards 8

The contractors who turn empty land into functioning infrastructure

Generating power is still just half the battle. Someone has to build the buildings, pour the foundations, pull the wire, connect the pipes, lay the roads, and keep it all environmentally responsible. When a new data center rises out of empty desert, a small army of specialty contractors shows up first. The Construction Yards is where that army lives.

Sterling Infrastructure (STRL) grades the site and pours the foundations, turning empty land into a functioning campus. Cardinal Infrastructure Group (CDNL) handles the wet utilities — water, sewer, stormwater — and the site work that has to happen before anything vertical can begin. MasTec (MTZ) runs the heavy power lines from the grid to the property line, plus the pipelines and cell towers that connect the larger network. Construction Partners (ROAD) builds the highways that get everyone from the outside world to the facility, because every data center still needs roads to reach it.

Inside the building, Comfort Systems USA (FIX) installs the HVAC, plumbing, and electrical systems while IES Holdings (IESC) handles the specialized high-voltage electrical work — wiring facilities that draw as much power as small towns. Legence (LGN) provides engineering, installation, and maintenance for the mission-critical mechanical systems inside technology, life sciences, healthcare, and education buildings. Applied Digital (APLD) takes it one step further, designing entire AI-native data-center facilities from scratch with electrical and cooling systems engineered for demands traditional buildings were never meant to handle. And CECO Environmental (CECO) installs the scrubbers, filters, and wastewater-treatment equipment that ensure all this growth doesn't come at the expense of the air and water around it. Before a single server rack spins up, these companies have already done most of the real work.

The Power Plant 4

Where the electrons come from — and where they get used

Intelligence without electricity is just an expensive pile of metal. And the amount of electricity that AI requires is almost hard to believe — a single large AI training run can consume as much power as 120 American homes use in an entire year. The existing grid was never designed for this.

Bloom Energy (BE) is building a bridge. Their ceramic fuel cells generate electricity through an electrochemical reaction — continuous, on-site, no combustion, no connection to the traditional grid required. A stack can be installed in 90 days and start generating power immediately, compared to the five-to-ten-year timeline for a new power plant. Nextracker (NXT) takes a very different approach to the same crisis: scale the cheapest source of new electricity ever created. Their motorized mounting systems let fields of solar panels follow the sun across the sky, squeezing up to 25% more energy from the same equipment, and have become standard equipment at the largest solar installations in the world. And Argan (AGX) does something more traditional but increasingly rare: it designs, engineers, and actually builds power plants from the ground up. As demand for gas turbine generation surges to feed AI data centers, Argan's skillset is suddenly in short supply.

But generating power is only half the equation. Someone also has to own the physical sites where those electrons get converted into compute. Three companies in this district were originally built to mine Bitcoin — which required exactly the same infrastructure an AI data center needs: massive power connections, serious cooling systems, and dense racks of processors. It turns out that infrastructure is worth a lot more to an AI customer than to a Bitcoin miner. Hut 8 (HUT) flexes between mining and AI hosting depending on which customer pays more. TeraWulf (WULF) does the same with a distinctive emphasis on clean energy, running primarily on nuclear and hydroelectric power. And Cipher Mining (CIFR) has leaned hardest into the pivot, signing a roughly $5.5 billion 15-year lease with Amazon Web Services to deliver 300 MW of AI capacity in 2026, plus a 168 MW 10-year HPC agreement with Fluidstack worth about $3 billion, plus a 200 MW Ohio site and 95% of a 1-gigawatt West Texas campus. Cipher is transforming into a hybrid Bitcoin miner and power landlord for AI tenants — and is increasingly priced that way.

The Software Layer 5

The code that runs quietly beneath everything else

Not every software company belongs in a futuristic city. These six do, because they build the scaffolding that lets everything else work — the cloud, the edge, the insurance backbone, the trades management system, and the business travel platform. Unsexy, essential, and increasingly AI-powered.

JFrog (FROG) manages what you might call the supply chain of software — moving code from a developer's laptop through testing and security checks to the servers that run it in production, making sure no malicious code slips in along the way. DigitalOcean (DOCN) is the developer-friendly cloud for startups and small businesses that can't afford the complexity of AWS; its AI-native revenue has been doubling for five straight quarters as AI startups look for a simpler home. Fastly (FSLY) runs a programmable edge network that speeds up the internet and stops attacks, concentrating servers in the most network-dense hubs rather than spreading them everywhere.

Guidewire (GWRE) is the operating system of the property and casualty insurance industry — more than 570 insurers in 42 countries, including Allianz, AXA, and Liberty Mutual, run their core claims, policy, and billing systems on it. The shift from on-premise to Guidewire Cloud is a multi-year migration that is now hitting full stride. ServiceTitan (TTAN) is doing for the skilled trades what Guidewire did for insurance — plumbers, electricians, HVAC contractors, landscapers — a full business management suite that lets a family-owned contractor compete with a national chain. And Navan (NAVN) is rebuilding corporate travel and expense management around a traveler-first experience, with $7.6B in annual gross bookings across 10,000+ business customers.

The Clinic 8

Finding disease before it becomes unbearable

Before there can be treatment, there has to be detection. The idea at the heart of modern medicine is deceptively simple: find disease earlier and everything changes. The Clinic is a small district but a powerful one, and everything it does flows from that one idea.

Guardant Health (GH) pioneered a way to detect and monitor cancer from a simple blood draw. When tumor cells die, they release tiny fragments of their DNA into the bloodstream — Guardant's test captures those fragments, sequences them, and reads the genetic mutations they carry. The result reveals not just that cancer is present, but what specific type it is and which treatments are most likely to work. No surgery, no biopsy needle, just a tube of blood. They can now even screen seemingly healthy people for early-stage colorectal cancer before symptoms appear.

Natera (NTRA) uses the same basic concept — reading DNA fragments floating in blood — for different purposes. During pregnancy, their test can detect genetic conditions in the baby as early as the first trimester. After cancer treatment, their test can find microscopic traces of residual disease months before a traditional scan would catch it, giving doctors the chance to act before the cancer regains a foothold. Adaptive Biotechnologies (ADPT) takes the idea even further: every infection, every vaccine, every cancer a person has ever encountered leaves a unique molecular fingerprint on their immune cells — and Adaptive's technology decodes those fingerprints to detect disease, match treatments, and understand immune response at a level previously impossible. Three companies, one mission: don't wait until the damage is done.
