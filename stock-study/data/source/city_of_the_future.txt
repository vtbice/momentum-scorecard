
The City of the Future
Somewhere in the American desert, a building the size of ten football fields hums with electricity. Inside, thousands of processors are stacked in towers that glow faintly blue, thinking thoughts no human mind could hold. Outside, fuel cells the size of shipping containers generate enough power to light a small city. Down the road, a factory stamps out satellite components on an assembly line. Across the country, a lab in Boston is testing a blood draw that can find cancer before a patient feels a single symptom.
These scenes aren’t science fiction. They’re happening right now, built by real companies that most people have never heard of. This is the story of 89 of them — and how, taken together, they’re constructing something that looks a lot like a city of the future.
Intelligence
Every great city begins with a brain, and this one is no different. The artificial intelligence revolution runs on physical things — chips, cables, cooling systems, and raw materials pulled from the earth. The companies building AI’s foundation are experiencing a wave of demand unlike anything the technology industry has ever seen, because every major technology company in the world is racing to build massive computing centers at the same time.
Start with the wiring. Inside every major AI installation, thousands of processors need to talk to each other at extraordinary speeds. Picture a room full of brilliant people trying to solve a problem together, but they can only communicate by passing notes. The speed of those notes determines how fast the group can think. CRDO (Credo Technology) builds the high-speed cables and connection chips that carry those notes — electrical signals moving data between processors at billions of cycles per second. They are the nervous system of artificial intelligence, and without them, even the most powerful processors sit idle waiting for information. Working alongside them, ALAB (Astera Labs) solves the same challenge at the rack level. When hundreds of processors are stacked together in a single cabinet, they generate an enormous amount of data traffic — imagine thousands of cars all trying to merge onto the same highway at once. Astera builds the intelligent traffic controllers that manage those data flows, prioritizing the most urgent signals, preventing collisions, and making sure nothing gets stuck in a bottleneck. Their technology has become essential inside the world’s largest AI installations.
But moving data is only part of the puzzle. Signals need to travel not just within a building but between buildings, between cities, and across oceans. That’s where fiber-optic networks come in — glass cables thinner than a human hair that carry information as pulses of light. MTSI (MACOM) designs the chips that make this magic happen. On one end, they take an electrical signal — the language that computers speak — and use a tiny laser to convert it into a beam of light that shoots through the glass fiber. On the other end, a sensor catches that light and converts it back into an electrical signal the receiving computer can understand. This electrical-to-light-to-electrical conversion happens billions of times per second, and MACOM’s chips are what make it fast, reliable, and efficient enough to power the world’s communications networks.
SITM (SiTime) makes something deceptively simple but absolutely essential: precision timing devices. Every electronic system needs a clock — a tiny heartbeat that keeps all its signals perfectly synchronized, like a conductor keeping an orchestra in time. If the timing drifts even a fraction of a second, signals collide, data gets corrupted, and systems fail. SiTime’s silicon-based clocks are smaller, more accurate, and more resilient to vibration and temperature changes than traditional alternatives, which is why they’re showing up inside everything from AI data centers to self-driving cars. LSCC (Lattice Semiconductor) makes programmable chips — processors that can be reconfigured after installation, like a toolbox that can transform into whatever tool you need at the moment. Traditional chips are designed to do one job forever. Lattice’s chips can be reprogrammed to handle new tasks as requirements change, making them invaluable in factories, cars, and communication equipment where flexibility matters.
SMTC (Semtech) acts as the quality-control system for electronic signals. As data travels through cables and circuits, the signal gradually degrades — like a photocopy of a photocopy getting fuzzier each time. Semtech’s chips clean up and restore those signals so they arrive crisp and intact, even after traveling long distances. Their technology sits inside data-center switches, smart-city sensors, and agricultural monitoring systems around the world. ALGM (Allegro MicroSystems) builds sensors that detect motion, position, and electrical current inside machines. When an electric car’s motor spins, Allegro’s sensors measure exactly how fast and in what direction, allowing the control system to adjust power delivery thousands of times per second. The same principle applies to factory robots, data-center cooling fans, and any application where something moves and needs to be precisely controlled. And RMBS (Rambus) designs the rules that govern how processors and memory chips communicate. Think of memory like a library and the processor like a reader — Rambus designs the system that determines how fast the reader can check out and return books. The faster that exchange happens, the faster the entire computer runs.
Upstream from all of this sit the companies that make chip manufacturing possible in the first place. Modern chips have features measured in billionths of a meter — structures so small that a single virus would tower over them like a skyscraper. Building something at that scale requires extraordinary precision at every step. NVMI (Nova) builds the inspection tools that chipmakers use to verify each microscopic layer during production, essentially photographing the chip’s surface with beams of light and electrons to catch defects invisible to any human eye. MKSI (MKS Instruments) provides the instruments that control the gases, temperatures, and energy inside the fabrication chambers — think of it as maintaining perfect weather conditions inside a sealed room where even a single stray particle could ruin everything. And AMKR (Amkor Technology) handles the final step: taking a finished chip — a delicate flake of silicon thinner than a fingernail — and encasing it in a protective housing with hundreds of tiny electrical connections, so it can survive the intense heat and vibration inside a working computer.
That heat is one of the biggest physical challenges of the AI era. A single rack of AI processors generates as much warmth as a small house, and a data center full of them produces heat like a furnace. Traditional air conditioning simply cannot keep up. MOD (Modine Manufacturing) designs advanced cooling systems that pump liquid directly past the hottest components, absorbing heat the way a car’s radiator cools an engine, then carrying it away to be dissipated safely. They’ve transformed from a legacy auto-parts company into one of the most important names in AI infrastructure.
And at the very beginning of the supply chain, before any chip is designed or any cable is manufactured, someone has to pull the raw materials from the ground. MP (MP Materials) mines and processes rare-earth elements — the minerals that create the powerful permanent magnets inside electric car motors, wind turbines, fighter jets, and computer hard drives. These elements have unique magnetic properties that no other material can replicate, which is why they’re called “rare” — not because they’re scarce in the earth’s crust, but because extracting and refining them is extraordinarily difficult. MP is the only large-scale rare-earth producer in the Western Hemisphere.
Finally, at the farthest frontier of computing, QBTS (D-Wave Quantum) is building quantum computers. Traditional computers store information as ones and zeros — like a light switch that’s either on or off. Quantum computers exploit a strange property of subatomic particles that allows them to be both on and off at the same time, which means they can explore millions of possible solutions to a problem simultaneously rather than checking each one in sequence. Drug discovery, logistics optimization, materials science — problems that would take a conventional computer thousands of years could potentially be solved in hours. It is early, it is speculative, and if it works at scale, it changes everything.
Power
Intelligence without electricity is just a very expensive pile of metal. And the amount of electricity that artificial intelligence requires is almost hard to believe — a single large AI training session can consume as much power as 120 American homes use in an entire year. The existing power grid was never designed for this kind of sudden, concentrated demand. Someone has to upgrade it, and fast.
That someone might be BE (Bloom Energy). Their fuel cells generate electricity through an electrochemical reaction — similar in concept to a battery, but instead of storing energy and running down, they continuously convert natural gas or hydrogen into electricity as long as fuel is supplied. No combustion, no turbines, no connection to the traditional power grid required. A stack of Bloom’s ceramic fuel cells can be installed on-site in as little as 90 days and start generating power immediately. Compare that to the five-to-ten-year timeline for building a new power plant, and you start to understand why some of the world’s largest investors are pouring billions into deploying them at AI data centers. Bloom is doubling its manufacturing capacity to meet the surge in demand.
But generating power is only half the equation. Someone has to build the physical facilities and wire them up. FIX (Comfort Systems USA) installs the heating, cooling, plumbing, electrical, and fire-protection systems inside large commercial buildings. When a new data center or hospital is constructed, Comfort Systems is the company making sure the air flows properly, the water runs, the temperature stays within precise limits, and the power reaches every room. They’ve become one of the most in-demand contractors in the country. IESC (IES Holdings) handles the specialized electrical work — running the heavy-duty wiring that connects buildings to power sources and distributing electricity safely through facilities that may draw as much power as a small town. WLDN (Willdan Group) works a step earlier in the process, designing the plans that cities and utilities use to modernize their electrical grids — figuring out where new capacity is needed, how existing buildings can use energy more efficiently, and how the transition to greater electrification can happen in an orderly way.
Then there’s the raw construction. STRL (Sterling Infrastructure) does the massive earthwork that turns empty land into a functioning data-center campus — grading the site, pouring foundations, installing underground water and electrical systems, and building the roads within the campus. MTZ (MasTec) is one of the largest infrastructure contractors in the country, building the power lines that carry electricity from generation sources to where it’s needed, the pipelines that deliver fuel, and the cell towers that connect wireless networks. ROAD (Construction Partners) builds and maintains the highways connecting all of these facilities to the outside world — easy to overlook, but every data center, every power plant, every factory needs roads to reach it. And APLD (Applied Digital) is building entire data-center facilities designed from scratch for AI computing, with electrical and cooling systems engineered specifically for the extreme demands that traditional buildings were never meant to handle.
The Builders
A city of the future needs more than electricity and data. It needs physical things — precision-machined parts, specialty metals that can survive extreme heat and pressure, and engineered control systems that keep jet engines running and power plants humming. These are the companies that transform raw materials into the building blocks of modern civilization.
SANM (Sanmina) and FN (Fabrinet) are two of the world’s most important contract manufacturers — the companies that brilliant inventors turn to when they’ve designed something remarkable but need someone who can actually build it. Sanmina handles complex electronic assemblies: the densely packed circuit boards inside medical imaging machines, the communication systems inside military vehicles, the server boards that run cloud computing. Fabrinet specializes in work that requires almost unimaginable precision — the tiny optical components inside fiber-optic networks that must be aligned to tolerances measured in millionths of a meter, thinner than a wavelength of light. They are the hidden hands that turn designs into reality.
XMTR (Xometry) has built something genuinely new: an AI-powered online marketplace for custom manufacturing. An engineer uploads a 3D design file, and Xometry’s system instantly analyzes its geometry, selects the best manufacturing method — machining, 3D printing, injection molding, sheet-metal cutting — calculates a price, and routes the order to the ideal manufacturer from a network of thousands around the world. Finished parts arrive in days. It’s bringing the convenience of online shopping to an industry that has historically required weeks of phone calls and fax machines.
The city also needs materials that can survive extreme conditions. CRS (Carpenter Technology) forges specialty metals and advanced alloys — materials engineered at the atomic level to withstand 2,000-degree temperatures, corrosive chemicals, and thousands of pounds of pressure. These go into jet-engine turbine blades, surgical implants, and nuclear reactor components — applications where a material failure isn’t an inconvenience but a catastrophe. RBC (RBC Bearings) makes the precision bearings that enable smooth rotation inside helicopter rotors, submarine propulsion systems, and industrial machinery. A bearing is a deceptively simple concept — it reduces friction between moving parts — but engineering one that operates flawlessly under thousands of pounds of force, at extreme temperatures, for years without maintenance, is anything but simple. WWD (Woodward) designs the control systems that regulate fuel flow and power output inside aircraft engines and industrial turbines. When a pilot pushes the throttle forward, Woodward’s technology is what precisely meters the fuel, adjusts the air intake, and ensures the engine delivers exactly the right amount of power without overheating or flaming out.
CR (Crane Company) supplies the engineered valves, sensors, and fluid-handling systems used in aircraft, naval vessels, and factories — components that control the flow of fuel, hydraulic fluid, and air in environments where reliability is everything. LFUS (Littelfuse) protects electrical circuits from destruction. Every time there’s a power surge, a lightning strike, or an unexpected spike in current, Littelfuse’s components sacrifice themselves — absorbing the excess energy and breaking the circuit before expensive equipment is damaged, like a fuse blowing to protect your house’s wiring. As the world fills with more sensitive electronics, that protection becomes more important, not less. And AGX (Argan) builds power plants from the ground up — designing, engineering, and constructing the generation facilities that create the electricity the rest of the city depends on.
Capital and Connection
None of this gets built without money, and in the modern economy, the flow of capital and the flow of attention are deeply intertwined. These companies move money, connect people, and power the digital platforms that shape how we live, work, and shop.
EVR (Evercore) is one of the most respected independent investment banks in the world. When two large companies are considering merging, or a fast-growing private company is ready to sell shares to the public for the first time, Evercore advises on how to structure the deal, what price is fair, and how to navigate the complex negotiations involved. They serve as the financial architects behind some of the most consequential corporate decisions in the economy. PIPR (Piper Sandler) does similar work with particular expertise in healthcare and technology, often helping innovative smaller companies raise the capital they need to grow from promising startup to established player. MIAX runs options exchanges — the electronic marketplaces where investors buy and sell contracts that let them protect against losses or profit from price changes. Think of it like insurance for investments: a farmer might lock in a price for next year’s harvest, and a money manager might protect against a market downturn, and both of those transactions happen on an exchange like MIAX.
FTAI (FTAI Aviation) occupies a fascinating niche where finance meets aerospace. Commercial jet engines are among the most expensive and complex machines ever built — a single engine can cost tens of millions of dollars and contains thousands of precision parts spinning at incredible speeds in extreme heat. FTAI leases these engines to airlines and, crucially, has built a deep expertise in overhauling and repairing them. When an engine comes in for service, their technicians disassemble it, inspect every component, replace what’s worn, and return it to like-new condition — a process that requires years of specialized knowledge and is extremely hard for newcomers to replicate.
On the platform side, RDDT (Reddit) hosts one of the internet’s largest collections of authentic human conversation. Hundreds of millions of users organize themselves into thousands of topic-specific communities covering everything from home cooking to astrophysics, generating detailed, passionate discussions that feel very different from the polished content on other social platforms. That authenticity has become surprisingly valuable — AI companies are licensing Reddit’s archive because training an AI model requires vast amounts of genuine human writing, and Reddit has more of it than almost anyone. U (Unity Technologies) builds the 3D engines that create digital worlds — the underlying technology that renders the graphics in video games, animated films, and increasingly, industrial simulations where companies design and test products in virtual environments before building them in the real world. FROG (JFrog) manages what you might call the supply chain of software. Just as a physical product moves from factory to warehouse to store shelf, software moves from a developer’s computer through a series of testing and security stages before reaching the servers that run it in production. JFrog makes sure that pipeline runs smoothly and that no malicious code slips in along the way.
KVYO (Klaviyo) is the engine behind the personalized marketing that online stores use to build customer relationships. When you receive an email reminding you about something you left in a shopping cart, or a birthday discount from a store you bought from once, Klaviyo’s software is likely powering it. Their system tracks what each customer has browsed, bought, and clicked, then automatically sends the right message at the right time — and once a business starts using it, they rarely switch away because all of that customer intelligence would be lost. PLNT (Planet Fitness) has made fitness accessible to millions of people who felt intimidated by traditional gyms, with rock-bottom membership prices and a welcoming, no-judgment atmosphere that has turned exercise from something exclusive into something everyone can afford. LTH (Life Time Holdings) takes the opposite approach — large, resort-style wellness campuses with pools, tennis courts, coworking spaces, and children’s programs under one roof, designed for people who want to build their entire daily routine around health and community. And MIR (Mirion Technologies) provides the instruments that detect and measure radiation — the monitors worn by workers in nuclear power plants, the calibration devices for hospital imaging equipment, and the environmental sensors that ensure radioactive materials are handled safely. As interest in nuclear energy surges, particularly for powering AI data centers, this kind of safety technology becomes even more critical.
Defense and Space
The city expands beyond Earth. And it needs to be defended. The future of military power is increasingly autonomous, increasingly digital, and increasingly orbital. These companies are building the drones, rockets, satellites, and precision components that are reshaping national security and opening the space economy.
AVAV (AeroVironment) builds small unmanned aircraft that soldiers can launch by hand — literally throwing them into the air like a model airplane — to provide real-time video of what’s happening over the next hill or around the next corner. They also make the Switchblade, a precision strike weapon that flies to a target area, circles overhead like a hawk, and strikes when the moment is right. KTOS (Kratos Defense) builds larger drone systems designed around a radical idea: make them affordable enough that military forces can deploy them in large numbers and accept losing some. Traditional fighter jets cost tens of millions of dollars each, making every loss devastating. Kratos’s drones cost a fraction of that, fundamentally changing the math of air combat. KRMN (Karman Holdings) provides the precision-engineered components that go into advanced military platforms — the kind of work that requires decades of specialized knowledge and security clearances that create very high barriers to competition.
Then there’s space, where RKLB (Rocket Lab) is proving that getting to orbit doesn’t have to be the exclusive domain of giant contractors. Their Electron rocket has become one of the most reliable launch vehicles in the world, carrying satellites to orbit with a track record that keeps improving. They recently won their largest contract ever to build a constellation of missile-warning satellites, and their next-generation Neutron rocket — designed to carry 40 times more weight to orbit — is targeting its first flight in mid-2026. If it works, it opens up an enormous new market. YSS (York Space Systems) brings assembly-line thinking to satellite manufacturing. Instead of building each satellite as a one-of-a-kind custom project that takes years, York builds standardized platforms the way a car factory builds vehicles — faster, cheaper, and in much greater quantities. The military needs hundreds of small satellites for communications and early-warning systems, and York’s approach makes that affordable for the first time.
VSEC (VSE Corporation) keeps military and commercial aircraft flying by providing the maintenance, repair, and spare parts that complex machines constantly require. Every flight hour generates wear that must be addressed, every aging component must be replaced on schedule, and every grounded aircraft represents a mission that can’t be flown. LOAR (Loar Holdings) manufactures precision aerospace components where they are often the only certified source for a particular part on a particular aircraft — a position that creates deep, recurring demand that competitors simply cannot displace. And MRCY (Mercury Systems) builds the computing brains inside military radar and missile-defense systems. When a radar station scans the sky, it generates a torrent of data that must be processed in real time to distinguish a flock of birds from an incoming threat. Mercury builds the hardware that does that processing, turning raw radar signals into actionable intelligence in fractions of a second.
The Healers
Even a city of the future has disease. But this city is fighting back with some of the most remarkable medical innovation in human history. This district is the largest in the city — 24 companies working on everything from detecting cancer with a blood draw to curing rare childhood diseases to keeping transplanted organs alive during transport. The breakthroughs emerging here have the potential to save millions of lives.
It starts with diagnostics — the idea that finding disease earlier changes everything. GH (Guardant Health) has pioneered a way to detect and monitor cancer from a simple blood draw. Here’s how it works: when tumor cells die, they release tiny fragments of their DNA into the bloodstream. Guardant’s test captures those fragments, sequences them, and reads the genetic mutations they carry — revealing not just that cancer is present, but what specific type it is and which treatments are most likely to work. No surgery, no biopsy needle, just a tube of blood. They can now even screen seemingly healthy people for early-stage colorectal cancer before symptoms appear. NTRA (Natera) uses the same basic concept — reading DNA fragments floating in the blood — for different purposes. During pregnancy, their test can detect genetic conditions in the baby as early as the first trimester. After cancer treatment, their test can find microscopic traces of remaining disease months before a traditional scan would catch it, giving doctors the chance to act before the cancer regains a foothold. WGS (GeneDx) sequences the complete human genome to diagnose rare genetic diseases, especially in children. For families who’ve spent years visiting specialist after specialist without answers, a single test can finally reveal the underlying cause and open the door to targeted treatment. And IRTC (iRhythm Technologies) has reinvented cardiac monitoring. Instead of a brief office visit that captures only a few minutes of heart activity, their comfortable adhesive patch records every single heartbeat over two weeks while the patient goes about normal life. AI then analyzes that mountain of data to catch dangerous irregular rhythms that a short checkup would almost certainly miss.
Then there are the companies creating treatments for diseases that previously had none. MDGL (Madrigal Pharmaceuticals) developed the first and only approved medication for a serious liver condition that affects tens of millions of Americans. The disease causes fat to accumulate in the liver, which triggers inflammation and progressive scarring. Over time, the scarring can become so severe that the liver fails entirely. Before Madrigal’s drug, the only medical advice was to eat better and exercise more. Now there’s a once-daily pill that directly addresses the underlying liver damage, with the vast majority of patients who could benefit still untreated. They recently received European approval and expanded their research with six new programs.
BBIO (BridgeBio Pharma) hunts for rare genetic diseases — conditions that individually affect small numbers of people but collectively touch tens of millions worldwide. Their first major drug treats a heart condition where an abnormal protein gradually builds up in the heart muscle like plaque in a pipe, stiffening it until it can no longer pump effectively. They then announced successful trials for limb-girdle muscular dystrophy, a devastating disease where muscles in the hips and shoulders progressively waste away and for which no treatment existed, and for a rare disorder where the body can’t properly regulate calcium levels. Both therapies are heading toward approval. For the families affected by these diseases, BridgeBio represents genuine hope.
Across the district, the work continues. INSM (Insmed) is developing better treatments for serious lung infections that currently require months of intravenous antibiotics pumped directly into the bloodstream through a tube, with harsh side effects. PTCT (PTC Therapeutics) works on rare childhood diseases where the genetic instructions for building muscle protein contain an error, like a typo in a recipe, causing muscles to gradually weaken over time. Their therapies aim to help the body read past that error and produce functional protein anyway. MIRM (Mirum Pharmaceuticals) treats children whose livers can’t properly process bile, a digestive fluid that backs up and causes agonizing itching, malnutrition, and potential organ failure. RYTM (Rhythm Pharmaceuticals) addresses rare genetic forms of extreme obesity caused by a mutation that breaks the brain’s hunger-signaling system. Imagine never feeling full, no matter how much you eat — not a willpower problem, but a broken biological thermostat. Rhythm’s medication restores that signal. TARS (Tarsus Pharmaceuticals) created the first approved treatment for a surprisingly common eye condition caused by microscopic mites that live on eyelashes and cause chronic redness and irritation in an estimated 25 million Americans who previously had no effective remedy. And GKOS (Glaukos) has developed tiny implants — so small they’re barely visible — that can be inserted during routine eye surgery to relieve the pressure that causes glaucoma, the leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide, replacing the burden of daily eye drops with a lasting solution.
CELC (Celcuity) takes a different approach to cancer. Rather than treating every tumor the same way, they test a patient’s living cancer cells in the lab to determine exactly which biological signals are driving the tumor’s growth, then match the patient to the therapy most likely to shut those signals down. COGT (Cogent Biosciences) targets a specific type of gastrointestinal tumor driven by a known genetic mutation, designing a drug that fits the mutation like a key fits a lock. Both companies represent a broader revolution in cancer care: treating the disease based on its molecular fingerprint rather than simply where it appears in the body.
In the research labs, the work gets even more ambitious. ARWR (Arrowhead Pharmaceuticals) is pioneering an approach that silences disease-causing genes before they can do damage. Many serious illnesses are caused by the body producing too much of a harmful protein — Arrowhead’s therapies intercept the genetic instructions for that protein and destroy them, like shredding a bad recipe before the kitchen can use it. RVMD (Revolution Medicines) is going after one of the most important targets in all of cancer research: a group of mutations found in roughly one out of every three cancers, including many of the deadliest forms. For decades, scientists considered these mutations impossible to treat because the protein’s surface was perfectly smooth, with no crevice for a drug molecule to grab onto. Revolution found a way in, and the research community is watching with intense interest.
NUVL (Nuvalent) designs precision lung cancer therapies engineered to solve a specific problem: drug resistance. Many targeted cancer drugs work brilliantly at first, but the tumor eventually evolves a workaround and starts growing again. Nuvalent’s drugs are designed from the start to block those escape routes, keeping the treatment effective longer. TERN (Terns Pharmaceuticals) attacks the same serious liver disease as Madrigal but through a different scientific mechanism, which matters because different patients respond to different approaches and combining treatments may prove more powerful than any single drug. CGON (CG Oncology) has developed a creative approach to bladder cancer: they’ve engineered a virus that selectively infects tumor cells, replicates inside them until they burst, and then spreads to neighboring cancer cells — all while leaving healthy tissue untouched. For patients facing the prospect of having their bladder surgically removed, this could be a life-changing alternative.
ACLX (Arcellx) takes a patient’s own immune cells, extracts them from the blood, and genetically re-engineers them in a laboratory to recognize and attack a specific blood cancer. These modified cells are then infused back into the patient, where they multiply and hunt down cancer throughout the body like a guided missile that builds copies of itself. Arcellx’s approach is designed to be safer and more precisely controllable than earlier versions of this therapy. ADPT (Adaptive Biotechnologies) reads the immune system like a book — every infection, every cancer, every vaccine a person has ever encountered leaves a unique molecular fingerprint on their immune cells, and Adaptive’s technology can decode those fingerprints to detect disease, match treatments, and understand immune response at a level previously impossible. ASND (Ascendis Pharma) has solved a practical problem that makes an enormous difference in patients’ lives: turning medications that require painful daily injections into formulations that work with just one shot per week, by wrapping the active drug in a molecular coating that slowly releases it over time. For a child facing years of daily growth hormone injections, the difference between seven shots a week and one is profound.
ABVX (Abivax) is developing what could become the first pill for moderate-to-severe inflammatory bowel disease — a chronic condition where the immune system mistakenly attacks the digestive tract, causing debilitating pain, bleeding, and long-term damage. Current treatments for serious cases require injections or hours-long infusions in a clinic. An effective oral pill would transform daily life for millions of patients. And TMDX (TransMedics) has revolutionized organ transplantation. Traditionally, a donor organ is packed in ice and raced to the recipient in a desperate sprint against time, with the organ deteriorating every minute. TransMedics built a portable system that keeps the organ warm, pumps blood through it, and maintains it in a living, functioning state during the entire journey. This extends how far an organ can travel, allows doctors to evaluate its quality before implanting it, and has dramatically expanded the number of transplants that can be performed. More organs available means more lives saved.
Some of these companies will deliver breakthroughs that reshape medicine. Some will face setbacks. That’s the nature of the work. But the breadth and ambition of what’s happening in this district is extraordinary.
The Everyday Economy
A city is ultimately about people. It needs to move them, clothe them, energize them, train them, and keep them healthy enough to show up tomorrow. These companies serve the unglamorous but essential needs that make a modern economy function.
SAIA (Saia Inc.) is one of the largest freight carriers in the country, moving shipments for thousands of businesses that need to get products from factories to stores to doorsteps. They’ve been steadily opening new terminals to improve speed and coverage — the quiet arteries of American commerce that most people never think about until a package doesn’t arrive. LYFT moves people, connecting millions of riders with drivers and increasingly partnering with healthcare systems to make sure patients can get to their medical appointments. BOOT (Boot Barn) outfits the American workforce with western and work-related boots, clothing, and gear, serving ranchers, construction workers, and oil-field crews through over 400 stores with a fiercely loyal following. FIGR (Figur) provides clothing and essentials to the growing skilled workforce building infrastructure across the country.
CELH (Celsius Holdings) has turned the energy-drink market upside down. While traditional brands competed on extreme caffeine and aggressive marketing, Celsius positioned itself as the healthier choice — no sugar, no artificial preservatives, clinically studied ingredients. The bet worked spectacularly: they’ve gone from a niche brand to one of the top energy drinks in America, becoming a genuine cultural phenomenon among younger, health-conscious consumers. BROS (Dutch Bros) is doing something similar with coffee — a fast-growing chain of drive-through shops where the employees are relentlessly friendly, the drinks are wildly customizable, and first-time visitors tend to become devoted regulars. With over 900 locations and an enthusiastic culture that feels more like a movement than a coffee chain, they have a long runway of new markets ahead.
UTI (Universal Technical Institute) addresses one of the most critical and overlooked gaps in the American economy: training skilled tradespeople. HVAC technicians, diesel mechanics, welders, electricians — you cannot build data centers, power plants, or hospitals without them. As the country embarks on the largest infrastructure buildout in a generation, UTI is one of the only companies positioned to help close the growing gap between the workers we need and the workers we have.
HNGE (Hinge Health) tackles the single largest cause of missed work in America: chronic back and joint pain. Their program works through a smartphone app paired with wearable motion sensors that guide patients through personalized exercises, track their form in real time, and connect them with a dedicated coach who checks in regularly. The combination of physical therapy, technology, and human support helps people manage pain without surgery or addictive painkillers. HTFL (HeartFlow) uses artificial intelligence to create a detailed 3D model of a patient’s heart arteries from a standard CT scan. The model shows exactly where blood is flowing freely and where blockages are restricting it, like a traffic map showing congestion on a highway. Traditionally, answering that question required threading a thin tube through a blood vessel and into the heart. HeartFlow can often provide the same answer from a simple, non-invasive scan.
The Hidden Backbone
Every great city has systems that most residents never think about — the water pipes beneath the streets, the air filters in the buildings, the wiring inside the walls. These three companies provide the connectivity, environmental protection, and electronic components that keep everything running behind the scenes.
CALX (Calix) builds the networking equipment that brings high-speed internet to rural and underserved communities. While the technology world focuses on the largest cities and data centers, millions of Americans still lack reliable broadband. Calix helps small, local internet providers deliver the kind of fast, dependable service that was previously available only in major metro areas, using cloud-based software that lets a small-town provider manage their network with the sophistication of a tech giant. CECO (CECO Environmental) designs and builds the systems that keep industrial operations environmentally responsible — the scrubbers that remove pollutants from factory exhaust, the filters that clean industrial air, and the treatment equipment that purifies wastewater before it returns to rivers and aquifers. It’s unglamorous work that ensures economic growth doesn’t come at the expense of the air we breathe and the water we drink. And BELFB (Bel Fuse) manufactures the electronic power supplies and connectivity components found inside an enormous range of essential equipment. Networking gear, military radar systems, industrial controls — they all need reliable power conversion and data connections, and Bel Fuse is one of those small companies whose parts quietly show up everywhere.
The Sun
The city diversifies its energy not only because it’s responsible but because it’s now the cheapest option available. Solar has become the least expensive source of new electricity in human history, and the relationship between clean energy and artificial intelligence is powerfully reinforcing: AI needs affordable power to grow, and solar delivers it at a cost that keeps falling.
NXT (Nextracker) makes the motorized mounting systems that allow massive fields of solar panels to follow the sun across the sky throughout the day, like sunflowers turning toward light. A fixed solar panel captures whatever light hits it at the angle it happens to be facing. A tracking panel adjusts its angle every few minutes to face the sun directly, squeezing up to 25% more energy from the same equipment. It’s a simple, elegant idea — mechanical arms tilting panels on a single axis — and it has become standard equipment at the largest solar installations in the world.
Digital Gold
At the edge of the city, two enormous computing facilities run around the clock, converting electricity into Bitcoin. Cryptocurrency mining works like this: specialized computers race to solve complex math puzzles, and the first one to find the answer earns newly created Bitcoin as a reward. It requires massive amounts of computing power and electricity, which is why mining operations look like data centers and tend to locate near cheap energy sources.
HUT (Hut 8) operates large-scale facilities equipped for this work and has increasingly begun renting that same computing capacity to AI companies. The infrastructure is similar — power connections, cooling systems, dense racks of processors — so when Bitcoin prices are strong, they mine, and when AI companies need capacity, they rent it out. It’s a flexible model. WULF (TeraWulf) takes a similar approach with a distinctive emphasis on clean energy — their flagship facility runs primarily on nuclear and hydroelectric power, making them one of the greenest mining operations in the industry. Both companies are expanding their AI hosting capabilities, recognizing that the same infrastructure built for cryptocurrency can serve the exploding demand for artificial intelligence computing. They are the city’s most unpredictable residents, rising and falling with crypto markets, but their physical assets have real and growing value regardless of where Bitcoin trades.
The City Keeps Growing
Eighty-nine companies. Ten districts. One city. From the processors thinking underground to the rockets carrying satellites into orbit, from the blood draws catching cancer early to the fuel cells keeping the lights on, from the specialty metals forged at two thousand degrees to the app helping someone’s back stop hurting — these companies are not isolated bets. They are a connected vision of where innovation is taking us.
The city is always changing. New residents arrive. Some move on. But the architecture endures, because it’s built on the forces that have always driven human progress: the hunger for knowledge, the demand for energy, the need to build, the imperative to heal, and the restless drive to explore what comes next.
The future is not a place we’re waiting for. It’s a place we’re building.
