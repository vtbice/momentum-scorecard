
Stock Write-Ups
AVGO — Broadcom Inc.
Big Picture: AI infrastructure — the company that designs the custom brains and networking backbone powering the artificial intelligence revolution.
Company Overview
Broadcom is one of the most important companies in tech that most people have never heard of. It designs custom chips (called XPUs) that companies like Google, Meta, and OpenAI use to train and run their AI models — think of it as the architect that designs the engine for each customer’s AI machine, tailored to exactly what they need. It also builds the high-speed networking switches that let thousands of these chips talk to each other inside massive data centers. On top of all that, Broadcom owns VMware, a major software platform that helps companies run their data centers more efficiently, creating a steady stream of subscription revenue alongside its booming chip business.
Growth Drivers
Recent metrics: Fiscal year 2025 revenue hit a record $64 billion, up 24% year-over-year. AI chip revenue alone reached $20 billion, surging 65% for the year. In the most recent quarter (Q4), total revenue was $18 billion with AI chip revenue of $6.5 billion, up 74% year-over-year. The company guided Q1 2026 AI chip revenue to $8.2 billion, roughly doubling from a year ago.
Market size: The market for custom AI chips and AI networking is enormous and still in its early innings. Broadcom now has five major customers building custom AI chips, including Google, Meta, and OpenAI. The company has over $73 billion in AI-related orders to be delivered over the next 18 months, giving it unusual visibility into future demand.
Key drivers: Three forces are converging: first, more and more of the largest tech companies want custom-designed chips rather than off-the-shelf options from Nvidia, which plays to Broadcom’s strength. Second, AI data centers need massive amounts of high-speed networking (Broadcom’s Tomahawk switches are the gold standard). Third, VMware’s cloud software is being converted to a subscription model, creating a growing, predictable revenue base of $27 billion annually.
Competitors: Nvidia dominates AI chips with its GPUs (graphics processors), but Broadcom’s custom approach offers customers more control and efficiency for specific workloads. Marvell Technology also designs custom AI chips but has fewer major customers. In networking, Arista Networks competes on switches but Broadcom supplies many of the underlying chip components.
Risks: Customer concentration is real — a handful of big tech companies drive most of the AI revenue. If any major customer pulls back on spending or brings chip design in-house, it would hurt. AI chip revenue carries lower margins than software because of component pass-through costs. Supply chain constraints, especially in advanced chip packaging, could limit how fast Broadcom can deliver.
Sound Bite: Broadcom is the arms dealer of the AI revolution — it doesn’t pick which AI model wins, it builds the custom hardware they all need to compete.
Bull Case
The AI spending wave is accelerating, not slowing. Broadcom’s $73 billion backlog is a sign that its biggest customers are locked in for years, and new customers like OpenAI are just getting started. If AI infrastructure spend doubles or triples over the next several years — as many expect — Broadcom sits at the center of every dollar spent. The VMware business adds a massive, high-margin recurring revenue floor underneath the explosive chip growth.
Bear Case
The stock trades at a premium valuation that assumes near-perfect execution and sustained AI spending growth for years. If the AI investment cycle hits a pause — even temporarily — the revenue deceleration could be sharp. Margins are under pressure as AI chips become a bigger share of the mix. And there’s always the risk that a major customer like Google decides to design more of its own chips without Broadcom’s help, shrinking the most valuable part of the business.

NET — Cloudflare, Inc.
Big Picture: Internet infrastructure — the company building the global network that makes the internet faster, safer, and ready for the age of AI agents.
Company Overview
Cloudflare runs one of the world’s largest networks, spanning over 330 cities worldwide, and more than 20% of all websites sit behind it. Think of Cloudflare as a combination security guard and express lane for the internet: it protects websites and apps from cyberattacks while also making them load faster for users everywhere. More recently, the company has become a platform where developers can build and run software directly on Cloudflare’s network (called Workers), which is emerging as the go-to infrastructure for AI agents — the automated bots that are increasingly browsing the web, running code, and doing tasks on behalf of people and businesses.
Growth Drivers
Recent metrics: Q4 2025 revenue reached $614.5 million, up 34% year-over-year — a meaningful acceleration from 27% growth earlier in the year. Full-year 2025 revenue hit $2.17 billion, up 30%. New contract value grew nearly 50% in Q4, the fastest pace since 2021. The company now has 269 customers spending over $1 million per year, up 55% year-over-year.
Market size: Cloudflare estimates its addressable market at $196 billion in 2026, spanning security, networking, developer services, and the emerging AI agent infrastructure category. The company currently captures just a fraction of this opportunity, leaving a long runway for growth.
Key drivers: The rise of AI agents is creating a new wave of internet traffic that plays perfectly to Cloudflare’s strengths. AI agents generate far more web requests than traditional users, driving demand for Cloudflare’s security, performance, and developer platform. The company’s Workers platform has become the go-to place for developers building these agents, and Cloudflare just closed its largest deal ever — $42.5 million per year — driven by this platform. Enterprise adoption is accelerating as large companies consolidate their security and networking onto Cloudflare’s single platform.
Competitors: In security, Cloudflare competes with Zscaler and Palo Alto Networks. In content delivery, Akamai is the legacy leader. Amazon (AWS), Microsoft (Azure), and Google (GCP) all offer competing cloud and edge computing services. Cloudflare’s advantage is that its entire platform runs as a single, integrated network — unlike competitors that bolt together separate products — making it simpler, faster, and cheaper for customers.
Risks: Cloudflare is not yet consistently profitable on a GAAP basis and trades at a high valuation relative to revenue. The company depends on continued enterprise adoption at scale to justify its premium. Competition from the big cloud providers is relentless, and there’s risk that the AI agent trend takes longer to monetize than expected. A meaningful portion of revenue is shifting toward variable, usage-based contracts, which adds unpredictability to quarterly results.
Sound Bite: If the internet is the highway system and AI agents are the new fleet of autonomous trucks, Cloudflare is building the toll roads, guardrails, and service stations they all need.
Bull Case
Cloudflare is positioned at the center of two massive, compounding trends: the shift of enterprise security to the cloud and the rise of AI agents that will flood the internet with orders of magnitude more traffic. The company’s growth is reaccelerating — not decelerating — and it’s landing bigger deals with longer commitments. If the “agentic internet” is even half as big as Cloudflare’s CEO believes, the company’s network becomes essential infrastructure for the next decade of computing.
Bear Case
The valuation is steep, pricing in years of high growth. GAAP profitability remains elusive, and the path from revenue growth to sustained free cash flow generation is still unfolding. The AI agent opportunity is real but unproven at scale — if it disappoints or gets commoditized by the hyperscalers, Cloudflare’s premium multiple could compress sharply. Insider selling has also been notable, which some investors read as a caution signal.

ANET — Arista Networks, Inc.
Big Picture: AI networking — the company that builds the high-speed highways connecting thousands of AI chips so they can work together as one giant brain.
Company Overview
Arista Networks makes the high-speed networking switches and software that connect everything inside the world’s largest data centers. If AI chips are the workers, Arista builds the roads between them — and those roads need to be extraordinarily fast when thousands of chips are training an AI model together. The company’s switches and its operating system (called EOS) are considered the gold standard by the biggest cloud companies, including Microsoft, Meta, and Google, which is why Arista has overtaken Cisco as the leading data center networking provider. Arista is expanding from traditional cloud data centers into the new wave of AI-specific facilities being built at a furious pace.
Growth Drivers
Recent metrics: Q3 2025 revenue hit $2.31 billion, up 27.5% year-over-year, marking the 19th consecutive record quarter. Earnings per share grew 25% year-over-year. For full-year 2025, the company is targeting roughly $8.2 billion in revenue with 27% growth. Management has set a 2026 revenue target of approximately $10.65 billion, implying continued 20%+ growth.
Market size: Arista estimates its addressable market at $105 billion by 2029, up from earlier estimates, driven by the explosion in AI data center construction. The company’s current revenue represents less than 10% of this total opportunity, suggesting a very long growth runway. AI-specific networking revenue alone is targeted at $2.75 billion for 2026.
Key drivers: The massive buildout of AI data centers by companies like Microsoft, Meta, and Google is the primary engine. These facilities require next-generation 800-gigabit networking that Arista is uniquely positioned to provide. The company’s new Ethernet-based products for AI (including its Etherlink distributed switch) are gaining traction as an open, standards-based alternative to proprietary networking solutions. Arista is also expanding into campus networking, chipping away at Cisco’s traditional stronghold.
Competitors: Cisco remains the largest networking company overall but has been losing share to Arista in data centers for years. Some cloud companies build their own “white box” switches using off-the-shelf components, which competes at the low end. Nvidia’s InfiniBand networking is strong in AI training workloads, but Arista is betting that Ethernet — the standard networking protocol — will win as AI scales. Arista’s advantage is its EOS software platform, which is lighter, faster, and more programmable than legacy alternatives.
Risks: Customer concentration is a real concern — a handful of hyperscale cloud companies account for a large share of revenue, and if any one of them pauses spending, results could be lumpy. Gross margins are expected to compress in the near term as more revenue comes from lower-margin cloud and AI customers. Long lead times for components (38–52 weeks) create execution risk. And there’s always the chance that Nvidia’s proprietary InfiniBand networking holds on longer in AI workloads than Arista expects.
Sound Bite: You can’t have an AI revolution without building the network to carry it — Arista is the company wiring it all together.
Bull Case
AI data center construction is a multi-year supercycle, and every new facility needs high-speed networking. Arista’s installed base gives it a deep moat — once a customer is running on EOS, switching costs are high. The 2026 revenue target of $10.65 billion implies the growth is durable, not a one-time spike. If Ethernet wins the AI networking battle against InfiniBand, Arista’s addressable market expands dramatically, and the company has the product lineup and customer relationships to capture it.
Bear Case
The stock already reflects a lot of optimism, and any disappointment on AI spending could trigger a meaningful pullback. Gross margins are heading lower as AI and cloud deals carry thinner margins than enterprise sales. The reliance on a small number of massive customers means revenue can be lumpy and hard to predict quarter to quarter. If Nvidia’s InfiniBand networking keeps its lead in AI training clusters, Arista’s AI revenue opportunity could be smaller than hoped.

GEV — GE Vernova Inc.
Big Picture: Power infrastructure — the company electrifying the world at a time when surging AI demand, grid modernization, and the energy transition are all colliding at once.
Company Overview
GE Vernova is the energy company that was spun out of General Electric in 2024, and it’s arguably the most important infrastructure company most people aren’t talking about. It builds and services the gas turbines that generate roughly 25% of the world’s electricity, manufactures wind turbines, and provides the grid equipment (transformers, switchgear, circuit breakers) that moves power from where it’s made to where it’s used. Think of it this way: as the world needs dramatically more electricity — for AI data centers, electric vehicles, and general electrification — GE Vernova builds the machines that make the power, the towers that carry it, and the systems that manage it.
Growth Drivers
Recent metrics: Full-year 2025 revenue was $38.1 billion, up 9% year-over-year, with strong momentum in its Power and Electrification segments. The company generated $3.7 billion in free cash flow and ended the year with $8.8 billion in cash. The order backlog grew to $150 billion, with equipment margins in the backlog improving by six percentage points year-over-year. GE Vernova doubled its quarterly dividend and expanded its share buyback authorization to $10 billion.
Market size: The global electric power market is entering what management calls an “investment supercycle.” Electricity today represents only about 20% of total energy consumption, but that share is projected to increase by 50% by 2050, driven by data centers, advanced manufacturing, building electrification, and electric transportation. GE Vernova’s installed base of over 7,000 gas turbines and its grid equipment franchise give it an enormous, recurring services revenue stream.
Key drivers: Three megatrends are converging: first, AI data center buildouts are driving urgent demand for new power generation — gas turbines are the fastest, most reliable way to add large-scale baseload electricity. The company’s gas turbine order backlog and slot reservations have expanded to 55 gigawatts. Second, the aging U.S. and global power grid desperately needs modernization, driving demand for transformers, switchgear, and other grid equipment. Third, management expects at least $22 billion in cumulative free cash flow by 2028, funded by improving margins across all segments.
Competitors: Siemens Energy is the closest global competitor in gas turbines and grid equipment. Mitsubishi Heavy Industries competes in large gas turbines. Vestas is the largest pure-play wind turbine maker. GE Vernova’s advantage is its unmatched installed base — when you’ve built 25% of the world’s power generation, you have a decades-long services relationship that’s nearly impossible to displace.
Risks: The wind business has been a drag — it’s been unprofitable and is expected to remain challenged while the company focuses on execution and selectivity. Large infrastructure projects are inherently lumpy and can be delayed by permitting, supply chain issues, or shifting government policy. The renewable energy transition could slow under changing political winds, affecting wind and some grid investments. Tariffs and trade policy add uncertainty to a global business with complex supply chains.
Sound Bite: AI needs electricity before it needs anything else — GE Vernova is the company that keeps the lights on and the data centers humming.
Bull Case
We’re at the front end of the biggest power infrastructure buildout in a generation. AI data centers alone could require as much new electricity as some entire countries consume, and gas turbines are the only proven way to add that much reliable power quickly. GE Vernova’s $150 billion backlog provides years of revenue visibility, and margins are expanding as the company moves past legacy issues. The company is also investing in breakthrough technologies like small modular nuclear reactors and carbon capture that could open up entirely new revenue streams in the 2030s.
Bear Case
The wind segment remains a problem child that’s burning cash and damaging overall margins. Large infrastructure orders are inherently lumpy — a few delayed projects can make a quarter look ugly. The stock has already rallied significantly since its spinoff, pricing in a lot of the good news. If AI data center buildouts slow or shift toward more energy-efficient chip architectures, the urgency for new power generation could diminish. Political and regulatory risks around energy policy add a layer of uncertainty that’s hard to model.

HWM — Howmet Aerospace Inc.
Big Picture: Aerospace critical parts — the only company that can make the superalloy engine components and fasteners that hold the world’s aircraft and gas turbines together.
Company Overview
Howmet Aerospace is the hidden gem of the aerospace supply chain. The company makes the incredibly specialized metal parts that go inside jet engines, airframe structures, and industrial gas turbines — components that must withstand extreme temperatures, pressures, and stresses that would destroy ordinary metals. Think of Howmet as the company that forges the parts no one else can make. Its engine blades, structural fasteners, and forged rings are found in virtually every commercial and military aircraft flying today, and increasingly in the gas turbines powering data centers. Boeing and Airbus can’t build a plane without Howmet’s parts, and engine makers like Pratt &amp; Whitney and GE can’t build an engine without them either.
Growth Drivers
Recent metrics: Q4 2025 revenue hit a record $2.2 billion, up 15% year-over-year, accelerating from 14% in Q3 and 8% in the first half. Full-year 2025 revenue was a record $8.3 billion, up 11%. Adjusted earnings per share grew 40% for the full year, and operating profit margins expanded by over 300 basis points to 30.1% in Q4. Free cash flow for the year was $1.43 billion. The company returned $700 million to shareholders through buybacks and announced a $1.8 billion acquisition of CAM to further strengthen its fastener business.
Market size: The commercial aerospace market has a production backlog extending through the end of the decade, with Boeing and Airbus collectively needing to ramp up aircraft production significantly to clear it. Defense spending is rising globally, particularly on the F-35 fighter program. And the gas turbine market is entering its strongest growth phase in years, driven by demand for power generation — especially for AI data centers. Howmet serves all three of these expanding markets simultaneously.
Key drivers: Aircraft production is ramping up after years of supply chain disruptions, driving demand for Howmet’s engine components and fasteners. Engine spare parts demand is growing as the global aircraft fleet ages and flies more hours. Defense spending is accelerating across the F-35 and legacy fighter programs. Perhaps most exciting, gas turbine demand is surging 32% year-over-year as utilities and data center operators race to build new power generation capacity. Howmet’s margins continue to expand as the company benefits from pricing power and operating leverage on growing volumes.
Competitors: Precision Castparts (owned by Berkshire Hathaway) is the most direct competitor in aerospace castings and forgings. TransDigm competes in specialized aerospace components, though more in aftermarket parts. Safran and MTU Aero Engines make some of their own components. Howmet’s advantage is that many of its parts are sole-source — meaning the customer has no approved alternative supplier — which gives it exceptional pricing power and customer stickiness.
Risks: Aerospace supply chains remain fragile, and any disruption at Howmet’s facilities could ripple through the industry. The commercial transportation (truck) market is weak and may not recover until the second half of 2026. Boeing’s ongoing production challenges could limit the pace of aircraft ramp-up, capping near-term demand. Tariffs and trade policy create cost uncertainty for a company with global operations. The $1.8 billion CAM acquisition needs to be integrated successfully.
Sound Bite: Every time a jet engine fires up or a gas turbine spins, there’s a Howmet part inside doing the impossible — and no one else knows how to make it.
Bull Case
Howmet sits at the intersection of three powerful, multi-year growth cycles: commercial aerospace recovery, rising defense budgets, and the gas turbine supercycle driven by data center power demand. The company’s sole-source positions give it pricing power that most industrial companies can only dream of, and margins are still expanding with room to run. The aircraft production backlog extends for nearly a decade, providing rare long-term revenue visibility. With 40% earnings growth in 2025 and another 10%+ revenue growth guided for 2026, this is a compounder hiding in plain sight.
Bear Case
The stock has had an exceptional run, and the valuation now reflects a lot of the multi-year growth story. Boeing’s production struggles are a real governor on near-term growth — if the ramp keeps slipping, Howmet can’t ship as many parts. The commercial trucking market is in a downturn that could last longer than expected. Integrating the CAM acquisition while managing capacity expansion is an execution challenge. And while rare, any quality issue with a safety-critical part could have outsized reputational and financial consequences.
