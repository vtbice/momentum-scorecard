The City of the Future

The companies in this portfolio are building the things underneath the AI and innovation narrative — the chips and cables and cooling systems, the power plants and transmission lines, the specialty metals and circuit boards, the drones and satellites, the diagnostic tests and rare-disease therapies, the trade schools training the welders, the contractors wiring the buildings. Most people have never heard of them. That is part of the point: in the gap between what the market has already priced and what these companies will become lies the opportunity we are looking for.

Taken together, these are not isolated bets. They are a connected vision of where innovation is taking us — a city we are building piece by piece. It starts with raw materials and silicon in The Chip Works, The Wires that carry signals between them, and The Machine Shop where precision parts and assemblies are made. From those components we build the city itself: The Power Plant generates the electricity that makes AI real, and The Construction Yards turn empty land into the buildings where it all runs. On top of that foundation come the services. The Arsenal defends the city and extends it into space. The Clinic catches disease early and The Research Labs treats what once had no treatment. Main Street feeds, moves, trains, and entertains the people who live in the city. The Software Layer runs the code beneath all of it. And The Financial District moves the capital that funds everything else.

Every company in this fund is playing a role. This guide is how to remember all of them.

The Chip Works 6

Where silicon begins — and where rare earths come from

Every city starts with a foundry. In this one, the foundry makes silicon. Modern chips have features measured in billionths of a meter — structures so small that a single virus would tower over them like a skyscraper. Building something at that scale requires extraordinary precision at every step, and the Chip Works is the district where it happens.

It begins in the ground. MP Materials (MP) mines and processes rare-earth elements — the minerals that create the powerful permanent magnets inside electric car motors, wind turbines, fighter jets, and chip-making equipment itself. MP is the only large-scale rare-earth producer in the Western Hemisphere, which makes it a strategic asset as well as a commercial one.

Inside the fab, Nova (NVMI) builds the inspection tools that photograph microscopic chip layers with beams of light and electrons, catching defects invisible to any human eye. MKS Instruments (MKSI) controls the gases, temperatures, and energy inside the fabrication chambers — imagine maintaining perfect weather inside a sealed room where a single stray particle could ruin everything. Amkor (AMKR) handles the final step, taking a finished chip thinner than a fingernail and encasing it in a protective housing with hundreds of tiny electrical connections so it can survive the heat and vibration of a working computer. Lattice Semiconductor (LSCC) makes programmable chips that can be reconfigured after installation — a toolbox that transforms into whatever tool you need at the moment. And Rambus (RMBS) designs the rules that govern how processors and memory chips talk to each other, which is another way of saying Rambus decides how fast the entire computer can think. Without these six companies, the AI revolution would be a slideshow.

Amkor Technology handles the final step of chipmaking: taking a finished chip — a delicate flake of silicon thinner than a fingernail — and encasing it in a protective housing with hundreds of tiny electrical connections, so it can survive the intense heat and vibration inside a working computer. “The librarian that decides how fast the computer can think.”

The Wires 6

The interconnect that lets the city talk to itself

A chip by itself is useless. Chips only matter when they can talk to each other — and in the era of AI, talking means moving vast amounts of data at extraordinary speeds between racks, between buildings, between cities, and across oceans. The Wires is the district where signals travel.

Credo Technology (CRDO) builds the high-speed cables and connection chips that let thousands of AI processors pass notes at billions of cycles per second. Picture a room full of brilliant people trying to solve a problem together, but they can only communicate by passing notes — the speed of those notes determines how fast the group can think. Credo makes the notes move at light speed. Astera Labs (ALAB) controls the data flows inside a single rack the way traffic cops manage a highway interchange, preventing collisions and making sure nothing gets stuck. MACOM (MTSI) turns electrical signals into laser light and back again so data can ride glass fibers across continents, billions of times per second.

SiTime (SITM) makes the precision silicon clocks that keep every signal perfectly synchronized — the conductor's baton inside every electronic orchestra. Semtech (SMTC) cleans up degraded signals so data arrives crisp instead of fuzzy, like preventing a photocopy-of-a-photocopy from losing quality with every pass. And Allegro MicroSystems (ALGM) builds the sensors that measure exactly how fast things are moving inside EVs, factory robots, and data-center cooling fans so control systems can respond in milliseconds. None of these companies make headlines. All of them are essential.

Intelligent traffic controllers for data flowing between AI processors stacked in a single rack.

Astera Labs solves data movement at the rack level. When hundreds of processors are stacked together in a single cabinet, they generate an enormous amount of data traffic — imagine thousands of cars all trying to merge onto the same highway at once. Astera builds the intelligent traffic controllers that manage those data flows, prioritizing the most urgent signals, preventing collisions, and making sure nothing gets stuck in a bottleneck. Their technology has become essential inside the world's largest AI installations.

“The traffic cops inside every AI rack.”

The same principle applies to factory robots, data-center cooling fans, and any application where something moves and needs to be precisely controlled.

“If it spins, Allegro is watching.”

MTSI MACOM Technology

MACOM Technology designs the chips that make fiber-optic networks work. On one end, they take an electrical signal — the language computers speak — and use a tiny laser to convert it into a beam of light that shoots through the glass fiber. On the other end, a sensor catches that light and converts it back into an electrical signal the receiving computer can understand. This conversion happens billions of times per second.

“Turning electricity into light and back, billions of times per second.”

SiTime makes something deceptively simple but absolutely essential: precision timing devices. Every electronic system needs a clock — a tiny heartbeat that keeps all its signals perfectly synchronized, like a conductor keeping an orchestra in time. If the timing drifts even a fraction of a second, signals collide and data gets corrupted. SiTime's silicon-based clocks are smaller, more accurate, and more resilient to vibration and temperature changes than traditional alternatives, which is why they're showing up inside everything from AI data centers to self-driving cars.

“The conductor's baton inside every electronic orchestra.”

Semtech acts as the quality-control system for electronic signals. As data travels through cables and circuits, the signal gradually degrades — like a photocopy of a photocopy getting fuzzier each time. Semtech's chips clean up and restore those signals so they arrive crisp and intact, even after traveling long distances. Their technology sits inside data-center switches, smart-city sensors, and agricultural monitoring systems around the world.

“Stops the photocopy-of-a-photocopy problem inside every cable.”

Woodward (WWD) designs the fuel-metering control systems inside aircraft engines and industrial turbines — what happens when a pilot pushes the throttle forward is Woodward's responsibility. Crane Company (CR) supplies the engineered valves, sensors, and fluid-handling systems used in aircraft, naval vessels, and factories. The Power Plant 6

Where the electrons come from — and where they get used

Intelligence without electricity is just an expensive pile of metal. And the amount of electricity that AI requires is almost hard to believe — a single large AI training run can consume as much power as 120 American homes use in an entire year. The existing grid was never designed for this.

Bloom Energy (BE) is building a bridge. Their ceramic fuel cells generate electricity through an electrochemical reaction — continuous, on-site, no combustion, no connection to the traditional grid required. A stack can be installed in 90 days and start generating power immediately, compared to the five-to-ten-year timeline for a new power plant. Nextracker (NXT) takes a very different approach to the same crisis: scale the cheapest source of new electricity ever created. Their motorized mounting systems let fields of solar panels follow the sun across the sky, squeezing up to 25% more energy from the same equipment, and have become standard equipment at the largest solar installations in the world. And Argan (AGX) does something more traditional but increasingly rare: it designs, engineers, and actually builds power plants from the ground up. As demand for gas turbine generation surges to feed AI data centers, Argan's skillset is suddenly in short supply.

But generating power is only half the equation. Someone also has to own the physical sites where those electrons get converted into compute. Three companies in this district were originally built to mine Bitcoin — which required exactly the same infrastructure an AI data center needs: massive power connections, serious cooling systems, and dense racks of processors. It turns out that infrastructure is worth a lot more to an AI customer than to a Bitcoin miner. Hut 8 (HUT) flexes between mining and AI hosting depending on which customer pays more. TeraWulf (WULF) does the same with a distinctive emphasis on clean energy, running primarily on nuclear and hydroelectric power. And Cipher Mining (CIFR) has leaned hardest into the pivot, signing a roughly $5.5 billion 15-year lease with Amazon Web Services to deliver 300 MW of AI capacity in 2026, plus a 168 MW 10-year HPC agreement with Fluidstack worth about $3 billion, plus a 200 MW Ohio site and 95% of a 1-gigawatt West Texas campus. Cipher is transforming into a hybrid Bitcoin miner and power landlord for AI tenants — and is increasingly priced that way.

“90 days to power a data center, not 10 years.”

The Arsenal 7

Drones, rockets, satellites, and the parts that defend and extend the city

The city expands beyond Earth. And it needs to be defended. The future of military power is increasingly autonomous, increasingly digital, and increasingly orbital — and the Arsenal is where the drones, rockets, satellites, and precision components that reshape national security and open the space economy get built.

FTAI FTAI Aviation

Leases and rebuilds commercial jet engines — a finance-meets-aerospace business with a deep repair moat.

FTAI Aviation occupies a fascinating niche where finance meets aerospace. Commercial jet engines are among the most expensive and complex machines ever built — a single engine can cost tens of millions of dollars and contains thousands of precision parts spinning at incredible speeds in extreme heat. FTAI leases these engines to airlines and, crucially, has built a deep expertise in overhauling and repairing them. When an engine comes in for service, their technicians disassemble it, inspect every component, replace what's worn, and return it to like-new condition — a process that requires years of specialized knowledge and is extremely hard for newcomers to replicate.

“A jet engine rebuilder with a moat built out of decades of know-how.”

MIAX runs options exchanges — the electronic marketplaces where investors buy and sell contracts that let them protect against losses or profit from price changes. Think of it like insurance for investments: a farmer might lock in a price for next year's harvest, and a money manager might protect against a market downturn, and both of those transactions happen on an exchange like MIAX.

“Every financial 'just in case' has to trade somewhere. MIAX is one of those somewheres.”

“If nuclear is coming back for AI, so is Mirion.”
